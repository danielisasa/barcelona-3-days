Before you go

Getting around

The Hola BCN! card gives unlimited metro, bus, and tram rides for 2–5 days. The city is very walkable — most sights are close together.

Book ahead

Sagrada Família, Park Güell, Casa Batlló, and La Pedrera all sell out. Book tickets online at least a few days before you arrive.

From the airport

Take the Aerobús from El Prat directly to Plaça Catalunya — fast, affordable, and no transfers needed.

Free museum days

Picasso Museum: free Thursday afternoons. MNAC: free Saturday afternoons. Hospital Sant Pau: free first Sunday of the month.

Barcelona Pass

Worth it if you plan to visit many paid monuments — saves time in queues and money on entry fees.

Day One
Modernisme, Gràcia & the Gothic Quarter
9:00 am
Sagrada Família
↗ sagradafamilia.org⏰ Mon–Sat 9am–6pm · Sun 10:30am–6pm💶 From €26

Gaudí's unfinished basilica is unlike any building in the world. Arrive early — the light through the stained glass is extraordinary in the morning. Take metro L2 or L5.

→ Book tickets online before you travel. A fixed number are sold each day.
11:00 am
Recinte Modernista de Sant Pau
↗ santpaubarcelona.org⏰ Daily 9:30am–6:30pm💶 €18 · Free 1st Sun/month

A five-minute walk away. This UNESCO World Heritage complex of Modernista pavilions surrounded by gardens is one of Barcelona's hidden gems.

12:30 pm
Park Güell
↗ parkguell.barcelona⏰ Daily 9:30am–7:30pm💶 €10 monumental zone · rest free

Gaudí's hilltop park with its famous mosaic terrace and sweeping views over the city. The monumental zone requires a timed-entry ticket — book ahead.

2:30 pm
Gràcia Neighborhood

Walk downhill into this charming, village-like neighborhood. Wander its squares — Plaça del Sol, Plaça de la Virreina, Plaça del Diamant — and visit Casa Vicens, Gaudí's first major building.

→ For tapas: Intrèpid de Gràcia or Bar Bodega Quimet.
5:00 pm
Gothic Quarter
↗ barcelonacatedral.es (Cathedral)⏰ Mon–Fri 10am–7:30pm · Sat–Sun variable💶 Free · €7 full access

Metro to Plaça Catalunya, then walk into Barcelona's medieval core. Explore the Cathedral, Temple of Augustus, Carrer del Bisbe, Plaça de Sant Felip Neri, and the beautiful Plaça Reial.

7:00 pm
Las Ramblas & La Boqueria
↗ boqueria.barcelona⏰ Mon–Sat 8am–8:30pm · Closed Sun💶 Free entry

Stroll down to the Columbus Monument. Step inside La Boqueria market to browse and taste. Find the Joan Miró mosaic set into the pavement along the way.

→ Dinner: Viana or Sensi Bistro. End the night with flamenco at Los Tarantos on Plaça Reial.

Day Two
Passeig de Gràcia, El Born & the Bunkers
9:00 am
La Pedrera (Casa Milà)
↗ lapedrera.com⏰ Daily 9am–11pm💶 From €25

Start on the Passeig de Gràcia — Barcelona's grandest boulevard. La Pedrera's undulating stone façade and otherworldly rooftop sculptures are Gaudí at his most inventive. Book ahead or arrive at opening.

11:00 am
Casa Batlló
↗ casabatllo.es⏰ Daily 8:30am–10:30pm💶 From €35

A few steps down the boulevard — dragon-scale roof, bone-shaped columns, luminous interior. One of the most beautiful buildings in the world. The interactive audio guide included with the ticket is genuinely engaging.

→ Also worth a slow look: Casa Amatller and the other Modernista façades along the "Block of Discord."
12:30 pm
Palau de la Música Catalana
↗ palaumusica.cat⏰ Guided tours daily💶 €22 guided tour

A UNESCO World Heritage concert hall — an explosion of stained glass, mosaics, and sculpted stone. Visit via guided tour or, ideally, a concert.

2:00 pm
Lunch, then El Born
↗ santamariadelmarbarcelona.org⏰ Mon–Sat 10am–12pm & 5–8:30pm💶 €5 basic · €10 with tower

Eat near the Palau — Tosca Palau or Casa Lolea. Then explore El Born's medieval streets, the basilica of Santa María del Mar, the Museu Picasso, and the Parc de la Ciutadella.

Santa María del MarMercat del BornMuseu PicassoParc de la Ciutadella
6:00 pm — sunset
Bunkers del Carmel
↗ bunker-del-carmel.de⏰ Daily 9am–7:30pm💶 Free

Bus V19 from Arc de Triomf to Ctra. del Carmel, then 10 minutes on foot. These WWII ruins offer the best 360° panorama in Barcelona. Stay until the city lights come on below you.

→ The single best viewpoint in Barcelona. Don't miss it.

Day Three
Raval, Barceloneta & Montjuïc
9:30 am
El Raval

A relaxed morning walk through one of Barcelona's oldest neighborhoods. Stroll Carrer Tallers for independent shops, ending at the Rambla del Raval — where Botero's bronze cat stands in the middle of the street.

10:30 am
Palau Güell
↗ palauguell.cat⏰ Tue–Sun 10am–8pm · Closed Mon💶 €12 · Free 1st Sun/month

One of Gaudí's most remarkable early works — extraordinary light filtering through the central dome, and fantastical ceramic chimneys on the rooftop terrace.

→ Book entry tickets online in advance.
12:00 pm
Port Vell & La Barceloneta

Walk Las Ramblas to the Columbus Monument and the old harbor. Grab lunch at the Time Out Market or head straight to La Barceloneta — fine sand, narrow streets, and the city's best fish and paella.

→ Best restaurants: La Peninsular or La Cova Fumada.
3:00 pm
Montjuïc by cable car
↗ telefericdemontjuic.cat⏰ Daily (seasonal hours)💶 ~€14.50 return

Return to Port Vell and take the Telefèric de Montjuïc up the mountain. Pre-book the ticket. At the top: Montjuïc Castle, a 300-year-old fortress with panoramic views of the entire city and coast.

5:00 pm
Olympic Ring & MNAC
↗ museunacional.cat⏰ Tue–Sat 10am–6pm · Sun 10am–3pm · Closed Mon💶 €12 · Free Sat afternoons

A short walk from the castle leads to the 1992 Olympic Ring — Estadio Olímpico, Palau Sant Jordi, Calatrava tower. The route ends at the National Palace, home to the MNAC. The free outdoor terraces have spectacular views.

9:00 pm
Font Màgica & Plaça d'Espanya
↗ Font Màgica⏰ Thu–Sat 8–9pm (summer); check site for season💶 Free

Ride the escalators down to the Magic Fountain. After dark, a free show of water, music, and light begins — one of Barcelona's most memorable experiences. Walk to Plaça d'Espanya for a final dinner.

→ Dinner: Kurai (Japanese) or Arrocería Las Arenas (Mediterranean).

Safety

Barcelona is a safe city, but like any major tourist destination it has petty crime. A few simple precautions will keep your trip worry-free.

⚠️

Pickpockets — high-risk areas

La Barceloneta, El Raval, and the Gothic Quarter are the three neighborhoods where pickpocketing is most common — particularly on busy streets and at night. Keep your phone and wallet in a front pocket or zipped bag, and never leave anything on a table or chair. Bag snatching from motorcycles also occurs on Las Ramblas.

🎒

On Las Ramblas and in crowded markets

La Boqueria and Las Ramblas are prime spots for distraction theft. Watch out for strangers who bump into you, spill something on you, or ask you to sign a petition — these are common setups. Keep your bag in front of you and be aware of your surroundings.

🚇

On the metro

The metro is generally safe but be alert at busy stations like Liceu, Barceloneta, and Diagonal. Hold your bag in front of you when doors open and close. Be careful with phones on the platform — snatching through closing doors does happen.

🏖️

At the beach

Never leave valuables unattended on the beach, even for a quick swim. Thieves work quickly and in teams. Use a waterproof pouch for your phone and cards, and leave passports and extra cash at your accommodation.

🆘

Emergency numbers

General emergency: 112. Local police (Mossos d'Esquadra): 088. Tourist police (for reporting theft): Rambla 43, open 24h. If your passport is stolen, contact your nearest consulate immediately.

Unlike the US

Tipping
in Spain

Service is included by law in Spain. Tipping is always voluntary — never expected, always appreciated.

🍽️
Sit-down restaurants
5–10%
Optional. Round up or leave a few euros for good service. Not expected at casual places.
🍺
Bars & tapas
Round up
Leave your small change on the bar. €0.50–1 per round is perfectly normal and appreciated.
☕
Cafés
Not expected
No need to tip for a coffee. Leaving a few coins if you stay a while is a nice gesture.
🚖
Taxis & Uber
Round up
Round to the nearest euro or add €1–2 for good service. Not mandatory.
🎒
Tours & guides
€5–10 p.p.
For free walking tours, €5–10 per person is the norm — this is often their only income.
🏨
Hotels & Airbnb
€1–2/bag
For hotel porters carrying luggage. Housekeeping: €1–2/day left on the pillow.
💡 Key difference from the US: In Spain, a service charge is legally included in restaurant prices. You will never be judged for not leaving a tip — it is genuinely optional, not a social obligation.

What to order

Barcelona
Food Guide

The dishes you need to try — and how to order them.

Starter · Catalan classic
Pa amb tomàquet
Thick bread rubbed with fresh tomato, drizzled with olive oil and salt. Simple, perfect, and comes with everything.
"Pan con tomate, por favor"
Tapa · Everywhere
Patatas bravas
Crispy fried potato cubes with a spicy tomato sauce (bravas) and aioli. A Barcelona staple — every bar has their own version.
"Unas patatas bravas, por favor"
Tapa · Bar snack
Croquetes
Golden, crispy croquettes — usually filled with jamón (cured ham) or bacallà (salted cod). Addictively good.
"Unas croquetas, por favor"
Drink · Pre-lunch ritual
Vermut
Vermouth with an olive and a splash of soda. A Sunday ritual in Barcelona — bars fill up between noon and 2pm for vermut before lunch.
"Un vermut, por favor"
Main · Seafood
Fideuà
Like paella but made with thin noodles instead of rice, cooked in fish stock with seafood. Born in Barcelona's port area.
"Una fideuà, por favor"
Dessert · Local classic
Crema catalana
The original crème brûlée — a silky custard with a caramelized sugar crust. Lighter and more citrusy than the French version.
"Una crema catalana, por favor"
Snack · Basque-style
Pintxos
Small bites on a slice of bread, held together with a toothpick. More common in the Basque Country but found throughout Barcelona too.
"¿Qué pintxos tienen?" (What pintxos do you have?)
Drink · At any bar
Cava
Catalan sparkling wine — Spain's answer to Champagne, made just outside Barcelona. Cheap, delicious, and the right thing to order in Catalonia.
"Una copa de cava, por favor"
Tapa · La Barceloneta
Boquerones & anchoas
White anchovies marinated in vinegar (boquerones en vinagre) or cured salt anchovies. A perfect match with cold cava.
"Unos boquerones en vinagre"
